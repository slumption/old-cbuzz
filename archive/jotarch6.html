date: 2nd February 1996

Another Friday night, more John Peel. Tonight John Peel is playing a pre-recorded interview with one of cBuzz's favourites Dave Clarke. If you don't know Dave then check his stuff out and rest assured you will be familiar with him by the end of the year (cBuzz prediction). I've faxed John Peel tonite as when I did so a couple of weeks ago he read my message out; I'll let you know if he mentions it.

So what has been happening in Brighton town? It's been one of those glorious weeks weather wise - pretty cold but sunny, sunny, sunny. It's been a joy to get out and about and it seems that the same feeling is happening across the town. The month-long post Xmas/New Year hangover seems at an end. The town once more buzzes with activity as preperations begin again for what will be happening in the town this summer.

cBuzz is relocating and should be safely incorporated into the new office suite by the time I write the next posting. My column in the monthly Impact magazine seems to be going down well - they gave me a full half page this month (I got my copy today). My writing career beckons.

On Wednesday I went to the local arthouse cinema (Duke of Yorks) to see Brighton Rock. A film I know (& love) really well. I'd never seen it on the big screen before and it only served to reinforce the fact that this is the medium in which to watch all films. Though if I stuck to that I would only see a very small number of them. Brighton Rock contains many street shots and, as I know the film so well, I indulged in trying to spot the locations. Brighton has changed surprisingly little in a lot of parts (and a lot in many!), and these are featured in the film. One significant change has been that of the Palace Pier in which the current owners, the Noble Organisation, demolished the (listed) Theatre at the sea end with assurances they would rebuild it. Hollow assurances indeed as the space previously host to the theatre has now become a fun-fair. I remember vividly spending mis-spent schooldays on the pier (yep, I should have been at school). The Theatre was derelict and many vain attempts were made to get in and explore. The front part, featured in the film, hosted (at that time) the 'Penny Machine' Museum; and it's a wonder that the propreitor never challenged us about our absence from school. Fishermen were also allowed to fish from the pier and it was one of the Noble Organisation's first moves to kick off those people partaking in this truly ancient harvest involving the sea.

John Peel hasn't mentioned the fax yet and I move towards signing off. The cBuzz Radio pages have been resurrected and if you, like me, are interested in all things radio then have a look and submit anything you have to say

date: 22nd February 1996

My last posting was somewhat brief. Lots to write but pressure to get on with other editing meant I finished it far sooner than I intended.

On returning from Manchester I took part in a Reclaim The Streets action here in Brighton. The idea behind RTS is for large numbers of people to occupy a car congested street for a period of hours showing how quickly the environment improves and what can be done positively in such an area. The action was well supported and good natured though the police seemed determined to adopt a confrontational attitude. The following is a letter I sent to the local paper (Evening Argus) which was printed (though edited) amongst many such supportive letters:

15th February 1996

I am writing concerning the "Reclaim The Streets" action in Brighton yesterday and the associated media coverage.

I was present and supportive of yesterdays' action and felt that, despite the police attitude surrounding the area, the protest was both good natured and well supported.

Upon arrival I was prevented, by the police, from moving through the police line formed across North Road. Their action at this time appeared to consist of enclosing and reducing the area of North Road being occupied by the demonstrators; this was the activity that resulted in pedestrians being stopped from walking the streets and any resultant loss to businesses. Many shoppers were advised by the police that the shopping areas in the vicinity were "out of bounds".

Whilst accepting that the occupation of this highway was an illegal activity the nature of the police appeared to be that this protest was to be ended by whatever means they were able to so do. The geniality of the crowd, in which many families and young children were in attendance, was constantly challenged by the continued aggresive attitude of many officers.

My belief of the nature of this protest, as in similar protests held in London and other towns, was to occupy the street showing how quickly the environment improves with the lack of cars and vehicles, and to create an alternative demonstration of the benefits of pedestrianised areas. The police reaction was designed to frustrate this end, though the demonstrators were equally determined to protest and party. I stayed in attendance until just before 4pm when I went to meet up with my son arriving home from school.

On returning to North Road the atmosphere and police activity had changed somewhat. The police were re-enacting scenes from the miner's strike as they formed tight lines and aggressively moved the protestors on. They seemed keen to do this as quickly as possible, maybe to clear the road before the 'rush hour'. By this time most motorists intending to use this route would have been aware of the action (Southern Counties Radio had been reporting on the events since the early afternoon) and would have prepared alternative routes. There was no real urgency to clear the protesters; the police, however, seemed to wish to provoke a potentially aggresive situation to meet their ends.

Your paper reports of "a hail of missiles" to which the police came under. Whilst not denying that one or two 'missiles' appeared to be thrown, the emphasis of your report would appear to challenge the validity of your reporters. As to the assertion of activists "climbing on to the rooftops of nearby buildings to throw glass bottles and cans" it must be emphasised that to all of us that saw those climbing on roofs (maybe two or three) none were carrying any 'missiles' (glass bottles and cans?) as their hands were too involved with scaling the rooftops to be carrying such items. And no, they didn't have rucksacks full of potential missiles!

In today's paper no mention is made of the Bouncy Castle erected in the street, a provision for the children there alongside many other such activities which were unfortunately not able to be realised (entertainers, bands, face painting, Candy Floss etc).

Yesterday's paper made great emphasis on the inability of one motorist to get quickly from Spring Gardens to Queens Gardens (an amazingly short distance for someone to be utilising a car for); indicative of the car-based society we find ourselves living in -- where news importance is related directly to the 'rights' of the motorist. It is to this that such illegal protests will continue as generations realise the damage the motor car creates at the expense of us humans.

I urge all concerned individuals to support future activities; this issue will not go away and it is a greater display of democracy to be involved than to make indications on ballot papers for party-chosen political candidates who have their party's vested interests at heart over the real views of the local electorate.

Brighton would survive as a shopping and entertainment centre with far greater 'car-free' areas than presently exist. I will contend that not only would it survive but that it would be more attractive to visitors who have to currently contend with our high levels of traffic.

The By-Pass, opposed by many, has been built taking much of Stanmer Park (bequeathed to the people of Brighton as a park!) and should therefore now be made to take all of our town's through traffic; this can be enforced with greater pedestrianisation. The issue of a tram system for Brighton should also be incorporated - such as the very efficient system in Manchester, which has led directly to less vehicles on the streets of Manchester.

But the issue doesn't end there. Reclaim The Streets means exactly that. The car has taken a stranglehold of our society and it is no wonder that there is major opposition to such road projects as the Newbury By-Pass. The whole issue of transport policy needs to be addressed on a national basis - and this will only take place if we, the people, make such demands. This means that actions such as yesterdays will continue.

As someone who will continue to be involved I apologise if such actions cause inconvenience to individuals. However they can be no greater an inconvenience than those the car creates:

-- the inconvenience of high levels of asthma (and related illnesses) in our children and the resultant medical costs and suffering;

-- the inconvenience of road accidents to the emergency services and those who are personally involved.

Yours
Jerry Thesport

Following this action many people felt that the police action was far too aggressive and a public meeting was held at the Brighton Unemployed Centre in which well over a hundred people turned up. Not one felt that the police action was justified.

I spent quite a while on the telephone in discussion with Charlotte Harris from Brighton Police Press & Public Relations office. She maintained that for quite a few hours the action was "fairly good natured" and they "wanted them (the protesters) to have the ability to protest". The police on the ground maintained liason with one of the protesters, who, apparently, informed the police (when it was beginning to disperse) that some of the protesters were not prepared to move on. The decision appeared to be taken to break the remainder of the demo up by force.

I left the demo at approx. 3.55pm and returned about half an hour later. In that time the bouncy castle had been deflated and packed away and very soon after the police forcibly moved in. I questioned whether, considering it must take a good ten minutes to deflate & pack away the bouncy castle, an adequate amount of time had elapsed to allow the demo to disperse. She maintained that this had been. A supposed 'verbal warning' was given to the crowd however the police themselves admit that this was "possibly not heard by the crowd" as the drummers turned up their volume as it was given! Shouldn't the police ensure the crowd had heard such a warning before adopting such strong-arm tactics?

Three Metropolitan police officers were in Brighton to advise Chief Inspector Martyn Bailey of the best way to police such an action. Apparently they were not in attendance on the ground but remained at John Street station. (why?). The only advice the Brighton Police took from the Met. officers was to remove any scaffolding poles that might be erected. This they did at the beginning of the action which resulted in someone falling off the poles, though not injured (from what I know), this could have been more worrying and the manner in which this was done should now be considered, retrospectively, quite ominous.

Brighton Police have no intention to examine their actions on this day though they will deal with individual complaints as and when they arise. Personally I am keen to see the whole events discussed at a Police Authority meeting and will be contacting my local councillors to ensure that this is done.

More has been happening in Brighton than just this event and I will cover more in due course - I just wanted to begin to get the records straight over Reclaim The Streets. If you wish to have an input on this then get in touch and I will endeavour to reflect all opinions on the day.
